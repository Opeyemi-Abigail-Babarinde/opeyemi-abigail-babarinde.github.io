How I Built a Notion System for
Project Tracking and Team Accountability

As project teams grow, managing tasks isn't the hardest part — maintaining visibility is. Here's how I built a system that changed that.

OB
Opeyemi Abigail Babarinde
Senior Project Manager & Scrum Master
March 20268 min read
TopicsNotion · Systems · Delivery

As project teams grow, one of the biggest challenges isn't just managing tasks — it's maintaining visibility.

In fast-moving product and design environments, information can quickly become fragmented across Slack threads, meetings, spreadsheets, and multiple project tools. Over time, this creates confusion around ownership, priorities, timelines, and progress.

While working across fintech product delivery and design operations teams, I experienced this challenge firsthand. We needed a system that could centralise information, improve accountability, and make project tracking easier without creating unnecessary complexity.

This led me to build a structured project management system in Notion designed to support project visibility, team accountability, sprint coordination, documentation management, cross-functional collaboration, and stakeholder alignment.

The Problem We Needed to Solve

Before building the system, we faced several recurring operational challenges common in growing product teams:

  • Tasks were tracked across multiple platforms with no single source of truth
  • Team members lacked clear visibility into overall project status
  • Documentation was difficult to locate quickly when needed
  • Meeting decisions could get lost over time across scattered notes
  • Feedback was fragmented across channels and hard to act on
  • Stakeholders needed clearer and more consistent project updates
  • Sprint progress was difficult to monitor consistently across cycles

None of these issues were caused by a lack of capability within the team. The challenge was largely operational: information was too distributed.

"We needed a system that could act as a single source of truth while still supporting Agile workflows — without forcing the team into rigid structures."

Why I Chose Notion

I chose Notion because it allowed us to combine databases, documentation, task tracking, sprint management, dashboards, and team collaboration into one flexible operational workspace.

Rather than forcing the team into rigid workflows, Notion allowed me to build systems around how the team already worked while improving structure, visibility, and accountability. The goal wasn't to create a "perfect productivity setup" — it was to reduce operational friction and create clearer systems for delivery.

Building the Operational System

The system was built around a few core principles: centralised visibility, accountability, cross-functional clarity, flexible workflow tracking, and accessible documentation. Instead of treating project management as isolated task tracking, I approached the workspace as a connected operational system.

01
Centralised Project Dashboard

One of the first things I built was a centralised dashboard for project visibility. This dashboard allowed teams and stakeholders to quickly understand active projects, delivery status, sprint progress, linked tasks, project ownership, and ongoing blockers — without needing to search across multiple communication channels.

Each project served as a connected hub linking tasks, meetings, documentation, sprint activity, feedback, and project files. This significantly improved visibility across cross-functional teams.

02
Multi-View Task Tracking for Different Workflows

Different teams process information differently. To support this, I designed the system with multiple task views including board view, sprint view, timeline view, weekly tracking, monthly tracking, and list view. This made it easier for product teams to monitor sprint progress, leadership to track timelines, designers to manage workloads, and stakeholders to understand delivery status — without duplicating information across tools.

03
Sprint Visibility and Delivery Tracking

As Scrum Master, one of my priorities was improving visibility across sprint cycles. The workspace supported sprint planning, backlog tracking, sprint reviews, retrospective documentation, and delivery monitoring. This created a more structured view of work across delivery cycles while helping the team identify recurring issues and bottlenecks over time. Rather than relying solely on meeting discussions, sprint outcomes and learnings were documented and accessible for future reference — improving both accountability and continuity across projects.

04
Meeting Documentation and Team Alignment

Another operational challenge was maintaining alignment after meetings. Important context could easily get lost across Slack messages, calls, and scattered notes. To solve this, I introduced structured meeting documentation connected directly to projects and tasks. This system helped centralise meeting discussions, action items, sprint ceremonies, stakeholder reviews, and project decisions. The meeting calendar also improved planning visibility across teams while maintaining historical records of delivery conversations — especially valuable in cross-functional collaboration between product, design, and engineering.

05
Structured Documentation for Accountability

A major focus of the workspace was accountability through structured documentation. I introduced templates and systems for project briefs, sprint documentation, feedback summaries, task ownership, decision tracking, and progress updates. This reduced ambiguity around who owned what, what decisions had been made, what actions were pending, and what priorities had shifted. The goal wasn't excessive documentation — it was creating enough shared context to reduce confusion and improve execution.

The Impact of the System

After implementing the system, several improvements became noticeable across teams:

Outcomes observed
  • Project visibility improved significantly for all team members
  • Teams accessed information faster without searching across tools
  • Documentation became easier to maintain and locate
  • Feedback cycles became more organised and actionable
  • Stakeholder updates became clearer and more consistent
  • Accountability improved naturally across teams
  • Sprint coordination became more structured and predictable
  • Delivery timelines became more reliable sprint over sprint

Most importantly, the system reduced operational friction. Instead of spending time searching for information or revisiting previous discussions, teams could focus more on execution and collaboration.

Lessons I Learned

Systems Should Support People

Good operational systems should reduce cognitive load, improve clarity, and support collaboration naturally. The best workflows are often the ones that feel almost invisible — because they integrate smoothly into how teams already operate. If a system creates more work than it removes, it needs to be redesigned.

Visibility Improves Accountability

One of the biggest improvements came from increased visibility. When project status is clear, ownership is defined, decisions are documented, and timelines are accessible, teams naturally become more accountable without requiring excessive follow-up. Clarity improves execution.

Documentation Supports Agile Delivery

There's often a misconception that Agile teams should avoid documentation entirely. In practice, lightweight structured documentation improves Agile delivery because it preserves shared context across fast-moving teams. The key is balance: enough structure to maintain alignment, but not so much that it creates bureaucracy. Documentation should support collaboration — not replace it.

Key Takeaways

If you're building a project tracking system
  • Start with the operational problems your team actually faces
  • Build around how people already work — then improve the structure
  • Create multiple views so different roles get the clarity they need
  • Connect documentation directly to projects, tasks, and decisions
  • Prioritise visibility — it's the foundation of team accountability

Final Thoughts

Building this Notion system taught me that effective project management is often less about controlling work and more about improving operational clarity.

When teams can easily access the information they need, collaboration improves, accountability improves, and delivery becomes more predictable. Tools alone don't solve operational problems — but thoughtfully designed systems can create environments where teams work better together.

As product environments become increasingly cross-functional and distributed, the ability to design systems that improve visibility, accountability, and execution is becoming just as valuable as managing timelines.
